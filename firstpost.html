East Facility, Machine Learning Department, Room 19B, 7.4.26

22.3.2026

“This was The Dream, to come here. When I was a little kid.” They were watching the sun set far out over the bay. “To come here. You know, when I was younger, when I was this fat little Chinese kid who couldn’t get enough of sci-fi magazines, I used to write these weird sketches about these kids in a room that wasn’t anywhere near me, talking about things with substance, doing drugs in rooms of abstract art.” He gestured to the table that his sneakers were resting on, which was covered in LEGO plants. “And now I’m here, you know. And it doesn’t feel like anything. It feels like an office job. And you know the craziest thing? I can’t imagine what comes after this. Do you feel that too? Like I can’t imagine one second of the future that will come after this.”

    The boy sitting next to him rocked his head back and forth in acknowledgement. He was slumped far down in his chair, his hands shoved deep in his pockets, his eyes out somewhere on the middle air.

    “And I thought that there would be fewer tissue boxes. Less shoe scuffs on the paint. And the way that the carpet smells like old wool jumpers — have you noticed that? Like a wool fisherman’s jumper, immediately after long rain. Like a light wet dog smell.”

    The boy watched an autonomous car fourteen or fifteen floors below them pull up, reverse, pull up again, and drive off. There were a few people talking near the door of one of the engineering facilities. The sun, low over the waterline, was the colour of hot glass.

    “It’s so fucking dumb,” he muttered.

    “Huh?” The larger boy, whose name was Yinpeng, had been saying something about the architecture. “You said something?”

    “I—” The boy paused. He had said something, but he couldn’t remember what, because he hadn’t been paying attention. He hadn’t been paying attention because he was thinking. Or, more correctly, he was doing what he considered thinking, but which he expected to be quite different from what he imagined other people were doing when they thought. He did not organise or experience words in succession, as he had heard that other people did, as if they were taking notes on sentences emerging mysteriously from the black. Rather, his thoughts were evident to him at once, in parallel. More notably, perhaps, the manner in which they were so took the form of a giant white cube rotated in his mind’s eye. He had named this the Hypersphere, back when he had been too young to have a handle on the basic geometry. It went without saying that his skill for geometry had deepened—deepened, indeed, to a point where most people might have considered his abilities somewhat occult—but his basic affinity for the sci-fi-sounding name had stuck.

    He had not been paying attention because he was watching, with a sense of curiosity, a new theater of the hypersphere taking shape in response to his companion’s words, growing and pixelating out of the dimpled white rock and curious pink cacti into a shape not unlike a small tree.

    He knew its meaning precisely. Languaging the thing was more difficult.

    “I—” Multiple forms stood out to him in parallel, without linearity.

    The future directly growing from the past; the fundamental continuation of the present with past with future; the unremarkable nature of the future, then, in each moment it is received in; the sense of the future therefore as already somehow old, overly familiar; the sense of pure dread, or terror, in the embrace of a future that already feels familiar to us, a feeling of being coffined rather than cocooned; over and over, the attempt to escape by imagination or technology the snares set by this mundanity, and in response, the startling absence of surprise.

    He couldn’t language any of it, so he said: “I don’t understand time”.

    Yinpeng grunted, nodding knowingly. The other boy, whose name was Guarav but who normally went by G, pushed his hands into his pockets more forcefully.

    The sun and the horizon had met. The two boys regarded the cars, roads, hangars and trees that made up the East Facility Engineering department as the long low wash of sunset faded red to blue. There were a few split trash bins and white fire hydrants; some exploding palms and solid-looking darker oaks; further back, the anonymous laboratory hangars with autonomous machinery silently gesticulating; and above them, the purple, neon lit bay, and beyond that the satellite-stars.

    Yinpeng picked up a LEGO flower that had fallen from a branch on the table.

    “You know what it comes down to? What I find stranger than everything else?” He replaced the flower, though not, G noticed, on the correct tree. “Compatibility.”

    G looked askance at him. Perhaps he really had understood after all.
