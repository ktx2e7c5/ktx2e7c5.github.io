Manny and Adam

2023-08

The next morning, the sun on the horizon, Manny is out in the open air again. He is somewhere between Bridge Street and the river, where the houses start getting fancy-like. The air is cold or edible. It nibbles at his lips.

The bleary city materialises. There are cafes and restaurants with dirty awnings glittering with frost and ice-cube metal chairs and plastic topiary in battered pots. Pharmacies and hardware shops and clothing stores are boarded up and fringed with advertising, print-outs and litter. E-Cig stores' shop front signs buzz merrily; barbers brandish beard shave deals.

Manny receives little of it. He is wearing a cloud-grey puffer, a grey and rainbow-coloured Four Horsemen t-shirt with long boxy sleeves, a rainbow-candy neckchain and wide denim jeans over grey suede mesh-and-webbing sneakers which no one, on this cold morning, will recognise to be fashionable.

The air tastes like gasoline.

Last night curls inside him as chemicals, ghost movements. The drugs in his bloodstream leak out slowly, leaching into or bleached by the air. Where his mind was is a cacophony of post-pop static. His thoughts reach reflexively for plans and mathematics, but these refuse their handling and fret at the perimeter of consciousness. His mind's eye is on memory.

He gives in to its gravity.

The heart of his mind is the hole he'd climbed to, post-Sergio, to farm himself some dopamine. Zoo's low booths had suited him, dark, all blue and green LEDs, TV screens and plant-print drapery. He'd knocked back six multicoloured spirits without knowing why, and before he could investigate, he was looking for Cat. Then he was sniffing it on a toilet ledge, near a sink with dew-cold copper taps, with a dark man smelling of sandalwood and vetiver.

He worked in the music industry, he said.

The bars and pubs give out now, and Manny is into the terraced houses. Red and white brick, slick black tiling, shutters in the windows and parcels by the doors. There are hedges and door plants, a little withered by winter and frost. One or two chimneys silently pump steam. The road is long, and very empty.

At the end of the road, where the post-office might have been, is the Machine Learning Laboratory and Compute Center. It is made of glass, absurdly cuboid, and absurdly huge.

They took it in turns, ducking down over the ledge.

"What they've got wrong," the guy is saying. "They think the people want representation. They think the people here want to be seen."

He ducked down and came back up talking.

"People don't want to be seen. They don't want to be seen by anyone. They want the spectacle. They want to see."

Manny pulled at the lips of his eyelids in the mirror, inspected the clouds of greyish fluid near his sclera.

"Glitter-ball, astronaut, kaiser-kaleidoscope-3D." There was a little white sickle of Cat around his nose. "That's what the people want. You want to get in on this?"

"Mmfuckyeah," said Manny, quietly, his fingers on his eye, his pupils watching his pupils pulse, his thin veins throbbing like lightning.

He runs his card through a slit and enters the tall metal hall. The open-plan office is three stories high and ringed by plants and balconies. The air is cool and purified.

Manny notices the biosecurity team in the corner, tapping away. He raises a hand.

One PhD looks up at him through deep-bagged eyes and nods back.

He finds his terminal quietly, clears away some Coke cans, and boots up.

Then they were at the bar, drinking hyacinth-shade shots and spilling syllables. His name was Adam. His neck looked marked with claws.

"Lil Grammatica. Coffee Booster Rocket Ship. You know Pinball Wizards? Generated. They're all doing it. Ratfuck. The Minor Tour, Chrysalis, Arcadia. All the Uwu bands."

"Megatorch?" Manny didn't know much about post-human punk.

"Nah, they're kosher. They just use sound design and do a ton of cocaine."

Manny flipped the bar guy off behind the bar.

"Arcadia are the real shits, they're totally made — You wan' something?"

He took off his shoe, and before Manny could do anything, slammed it against the bar.

"What do you want?" he demanded of Manny.

The tattooed bar guy was moving towards them.

"You wanna get out of here?" said Manny.

There was something wonderful in the before-time, Manny thought, looking up at the plant-filled balconies and the brightening skylights above. Before everyone woke up, before the day began.

He liked to know about a place before it opened up.

When he was a kid, he had once stayed out too late, only to see fireworks exploding over the lake outside the village. In the morning, he had got up at dawn to pick through the rubble and charcoal with a stick. They'd set a tree on fire, whoever it had been, and the branches had blackened in the blaze. It stood out in the ashen light like a negative photograph of a flame.

It reminded him of the conjoined bodies in a porn video he'd once watched. Like an image you were not meant to see.

From then, time seemed to wheel and blur. They'd boarded a black cab back to the city edge, where they'd stumbled out to a block of flats with a camera drone over the gate and an eyescanner beside the door. He'd kissed Adam there, the light still inside his eye, and they'd tumbled through the sliding door or otherwise found themselves at the foot of the stairs breathless and hungry for it. They'd snogged in the lift a bit, and maybe he'd tried to get undressed. He didn't feel bad for that. Then they'd poured out in the direction of a big white door, an opening, a thick rug, then the long, low couch.

"You gotta?" Adam was saying, somewhere. Manny couldn't remember what he replied. He'd lain there, fingers splayed, looking up at the ceiling light. It had eight spokes, like spider's legs, with a sparkler pulsating at each end.

He logged in. He kicked through a backlog of model errors an AI had assembled for him, to ensure all was going okay, then moved over to his experiments. This was a routine experiment, little more than a task. The only reason he was up so early was because he had failed to do it earlier in the week. The compute scheduling programme was exhausting. Even though he was up at sunrise, he'd probably only have a few minutes to run those on his list.

He flicked through the long list of experiments with a lazy eye. Really, he knew what he was searching for. He already knew the function wouldn't run.

He found the file of pre-prepared code, and had the AI whip up a couple of evaluation environments. He found a suitable small model, one of the legacy Blueberry Pi ones, online and imported it.

A warning popped up.

'Running this training process can only be confirmed by human verification. Do you want to go ahead?'

Manny bio-identified and spoke into the microphone softly. His throat hurt.

"Yes," he said. "Yes I do."

The machine thought for a second, then agreed.

After they'd fucked for a bit, Adam put Kiosk on. He lit up a packet of scented cigarettes that smelt like incense and smoked like gravel.

"You should come to a concert. One of my new guys. Not a virtual one. A real one."

"Why would I go to a concert of yours?"

They fought for a bit, jokingly, then flicked the butts across the room towards a bin shaped like a trepanned buddha's head and made love. More songs by Kiosk played, always almost familiar. The sleek room smelt like cinnamon.

"What do you do?" Adam asked, eventually.

"I'm a researcher. At the Uni."

"I know. But what do you actually do?"

Manny told him.

Adam rubbed his neck, drew the sheets back over him.

"Fuck," he said.

The experiment was online. Already, more people were trickling into the laboratory. Some of the biosecurity people were beginning to leave. Something in the way they always looked at each other reminded him of that blackened tree. More were coming in through the door now, security-looking people. Seeing what you were not meant to see.

Manny ran his hand absentmindedly over the matte plastic desktop, along his arm, across his brow. He was surprised to realise he was wearing headphones. There was music, he realised. Music in his ears.

Little over two minutes of the experiment remaining.

Adam had turned the Kiosk off. Manny was standing in the doorway.

"This was a bad idea. I knew this was a bad idea."

"Don't take it personally," Adam was saying. "I didn't mean you to take it personally."

"Oh? I'm betraying my fucking species am I, but you, with your fucking slop farms, you're some kind of… some kind of…"

He could feel the Cat easing up on his temples. Adam looked genuinely sad.

"I'm sorry," he said. "It's the Cat, I'm not…"

"It's okay." Adam sat down on the bed, and sighed a little.

"I really did love music," he said, quietly.

Lying there in the dark, Manny had tried to think about how to describe what he actually did. Grand sentences bubbled up from the darkness in him, glinting gold. They came up to his throat and coated his tongue, but he couldn't seem to work out how to articulate what they meant.

As he fell asleep, a scene began to crystallise in his mind. He was with a child — maybe himself when he'd been a small boy, maybe someone yet to be born. They were sitting around the table in his old house, in the big room, with the wind leaking through the boards in the wall.

    What do you do? The child asked.

    I'm a coder.

    What does that mean?

    I make machines intelligent.

    What does that mean?

    It makes intelligence cheaper. It helps people to do useful things faster. The present starts to become more like the future.

    What does that mean?

The sunlight in the dream was bright enough to see the dust in the playroom, falling through the air.

You could play that game for ever, he thought to himself, his mind darkening. On and on, down and down. A universe of floating points.

It was beginning to get lighter now. People were filling the desks, nodding, giving small waves. There was a murmur on the air. Some new research, some new breakthrough, some new protest.

What was the concert that guy had talked about?

The experiment report pinged him. He clicked 'accept'.

Loading, loading.

That was it. Orpheus. The DJ. After the poet or something. The man who raised the dead.

(The couch, the bed, the nightshade…)

The results were coming back in, now. The hieroglyphs were red, then orange, then… green?

Manny gulped.

The host of Encyclopaedia of Sound was on the radio, live in Manny's eardrums:

"Coming up next, an album for the ages. You're listening to E.O.S. Next up, Tago Mago, it's CAN."
