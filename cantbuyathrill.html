Can't Buy a Thrill

2023-08

Three days later, Sergio Biscuits is cruising on a citrus-green bike through the empty October streets. Neither hand is on the handlebar; one runs a thin green felt tip through the air beside him, feeling the airflow on the outside edge of his palm. The intro to Steely Dan's Reelin' in the Years ricochets in his cheap earbuds.

Your everlasting summer…

He swerves off the main road and heads east, down towards the open fields.

You can see it fading fast —

All around him, the field breathes in lugwort, saper's fern and mistlerush, littered bushels of neon-yellow cowpox, Clary sage and hyacinths. Ferns climb and distill in fractal line vectors, tapering into singular whorls.

There is an Eduardo Paolozzi painting, from a more chaotic time deep in Sergio's memory, that conjures up the helterskelter of sprocket-ferns like these, pushing their noses through the cubic fence.

But on second thoughts, it does not capture the glint of the grit on the road. It does not capture the smell of the base of the plants, a lemony, warm-mist mulch. It does not capture the way that the stratus-cloud Cloth of Gold and lemur-tail Eremurus look impossibly futuristic against the steel fences and the dull autumnal earth.

The art does not exist, he decides. Couldn't exist if it tried.

Sergio slaps his bike against a lowered chain mesh fence here, unloads a sliver of nettle-green spearmint mosrophopedol from a wrapper in his right hand, and places it on the centre-left of his tongue. For luck, he thinks. He ups the music another notch.

Then he steps up onto the seat of the bicycle with one foot, then another, perches, and levers himself over the fence.

Are you reelin' in the years?

What had that dude been smoking last night? He reached for it in the vape smoke and whirling lights of his memory.

Stowing away the time?

All over the world, the boy in the blue top was saying, there were wormholes, wombs where the future incarnated. Epicentres, impact craters, patient zeros. Time machines, the signs of things to come. Strange things were tumbling out of them, machines humanity did not understand.

Are you gathering up the tears?

He scaled a shrub and limboed a bushel of cowfern.

The marks of an old path were meaningless here, in this broken-bracken bracket of forest and burnt earth.

Have you had enough of mine?

Written on these machines, the boy explained, were hieroglyphs. Runes on the nature of time. Time was the great forward pass, the motion of one state to another in the calculation of machines. The earth did not move around the sun; it updated in space at the speed of reality, a cascade developing as time.

The things that pass for knowledge —

He brushed through a turret of cow parsnip.

The thing about this reality, the boy explained, was you could accelerate it. Anything you could simulate, you could speed up. Everything was waiting for us, out there in the future. Even physics was not incorrigible, but could be distorted in selective moves, moving the impossible within the realm of the real.

He trod on a mound of smashed plums, sending a swarm of flies racing in all directions.

The instruments were beginning to blend with each other, but the lyrics were still distinct.

The things you think are useless —

But the imaginable? The boy was saying. The imaginable was a light cone for cognition limited by the processing power of our minds. Soon, these too would be irrelevant, the light cone would be open to them. Think of the cheddar cheese.

What?

Are you reelin' in the years?

Where was he?

Stowing away the time?

He felt the equipment starting to kick in, and he gave into it, let it carry him beyond language.

Have you had enough of mine?

No one saw the young man in the yellow and green Daffodil Satellite t-shirt and tawny brown pants step out behind the high-security Chemistry Laboratory and brush himself clear of bracken bits.
