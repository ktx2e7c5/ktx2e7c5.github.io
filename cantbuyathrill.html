Can't Buy a Thrill

2023-08

Sergio is listening to Chamber Four by Wagner. He runs a thin green felt tip through the air beside him, feeling the airflow on the outside edge of his palm. He is cycling upright handlessly through the clear October streets. His bicycle is the colour of citrusine.

Around him a part of lugwort, saper's fern and mistlerush rush past and distract the ground and bunch in bushels of litter-neon cowpox, hernan's sage and crystalline sprockets of nestle-blue hyacinths. Ferns evaporate and distill themselves into fractalline vectors, tapering sequentially around a single node. There is an Eduardo Vallance painting, The Heir to the Hero, that conjures up the helterskelter of a sprocket fern like this, but it does not capture the smell of the base of the plant, a lemony, warm-mist mulch. It does not capture the way the Cloth of Gold like a stratus scab and eremurus like an alien's tail erupt from the cumulonimbus of the bracket and spill forth their leafy hands through the apertures of the green iron fence. This is what summer is joyous for, in the paintings by Poussin. He slaps his bike against them and skips three tracks, unleafing a sliver of nettle-green spearmint sertraline from a wrapper in his right hand. Places it on the centre-left of his tongue. Drops a pair of watermelon-pattern children's sunglasses from his white hair shock forehead onto his bridge.

Sergio has arrived.

He steps up onto the seat of the bicycle with one foot, then another, perches, and levers himself over the fence.

Sergio is an archivist, an anarchist, a journalist. He thinks of himself as an entrepreneur. The future, you see, was already here, it just wasn't widely distributed yet. All over the world, there were wormholes where the future was being made, just like all over the world there were lines where it was being unmade. There were lines where the future would never go and places where, for all intents and purposes, it had long been the case. He climbs over a shrub and limbers under a bushel of cowfern. (The past is here too, of course, buried beneath the bracken). The decade cycle, the 24-hour season, these were antique relics of a temporally unified world. They were relics of a time when the seasons moved with the changing of the temperature which happened on an annual basis, where human body clocks emerged with the rising and falling of the sun, where animals migrated to the patterns of fields undistorted by the atmosphere on their annual voyages, where the chronotypes of mammals threw them into hibernation, through humans into sleep. Where you couldn't obtain the fruit of another season, where you couldn't outrace the spin of the earth, where you couldn't sit outside and watch a day emerge, where you couldn't reverse the ageing of a cell… He was fascinated by the places where Old Time hid. But he had been born in a different time.

The future was here, it just wasn't widely distributed yet. It was hiding, it was nascent, numinous, emergent froth. You didn't just build the future, you found it, like an archaeologist. Time occurred as an interactive force, implied evolving iterations. He was that catalyst. Had he been born here? The sunlight was brightest here, underneath the overgreen lightcone, where little galaxies of moted dust filter and explore the air indifferent to rules of gravity, like bubbles in water.

He wondered how to phrase this for funding.

What was this? This city was a time machine. The perimeters of the city were isochrones. Money was a time machine. No — GPUs were time machines. Time emerged as an evolution of mathematical processes at the smallest possible level, the physics of the nonsimulation playing through. This was Heidegger's nemesis: an infinite Turing machine would be a description of a clock. Psychedelic Symposium is an album by Benchmark, he makes a note and saves it to later. There are smashed plums caramelising in the summer's heat. There are snapped twigs rekindling in the lower brackens. He was a pilot. He was Sherlock Holmes, constituting the murder from the murder weapon. Author, conspirator, architect, all-seer.

No one watches the young man in the Daffodil Satellite t-shirt in yellow and green and tawny brown pants step out from beneath a pile of bracken and brush himself down. Can't Buy a Thrill ricochets in the cheap earbuds.

Sergio pulls out a pair of red-glass children's plastic binoculars and puts them to his eyes.

It's another tag, alright. This time on the back of the Biochemistry and Pharmaceuticals Research Lab.

There's about fifty metres of rapeseed between him and a closer look. He pulls out a brown-lined notebook and writes down with the green pen in ferociously tiny scrawl a string of words ending with 'hyacinth lycanthropy masochism attitude'. The words carry an energy like the sticks of the I Ching: he will formalise and tweet the sentiment as it emerges when it does so, his own personal time portal field. Any Dude Will Tell You is being injected into his left eardrum, since only one of the earbuds is currently working. Any minor world that breaks apart falls together again, the singer-songwriter is audibly noting.

He raises the binoculars again. Is this how a lake feels, he wonders, to absorb and refract sunlight?

It's definitely the same group, he concludes. Same style, same paints, same signatures. Only this time, a different phrase.

N-O-T-H-I-N-G-I-S-F-A-I-R-I-N-T-H-I-S-W-O-R-L-D-O-F-M-A-D-N-E-S-S!

Interesting. He takes out his phone, a Nokia Digital, and takes three pictures.

The information spreads all over the world, via satellites, in strange streams. Time reaches space first, then the cortexes of millions of operatives irregardless of space. It is diffused among the population at a semi-random variable conditioned by hundreds of algorithms, biological and digital, in interaction.

It will continue to spread on, in real time, long after Sergio has died.

Originally written August 2023.
