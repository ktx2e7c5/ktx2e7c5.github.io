That Good Night: Omen Ventures Profile (Cut)

2025-08-24

There is a characteristic Omen prides themselves on, which is making people look more real than real. There's a softness and an organic hemp feel to their surfaces. Characters move through dappled light elegantly, even as they are controlled by the hand gestures of an analogous creature in a simulation. The people are exceptionally beautiful, and quietly flawed. The selection of human masks is still done artisanally, in house, by a small woman called Liu Feng, who sculpted pottery in a previous life. She says, the hardest part is animating the AI bodies with an AI system and watching how they move in different personalities. It's emotionally challenging, like seeing someone you know go through so many different personalities, like imposing schizophrenia on them. How does it feel to puppeteer them as a player, I ask. Oh, people don't seem to mind that, she says. It's a thrill.

As a child, I would sometimes have dreams about hypnosis: those lucid dreams where I could control everyone, everywhere. These simulations give the opportunity to do that. A quick press of a button can transfer you, the controller, into the consciousness of another character. You can control them in third or first person view.

Why first person view, I ask. It's the same for everyone?

That's the point, she replies. I think that's interesting. But the point of view: literally, the height, the way the head moves — these things are different. I think that's important. You're not there, on the screen, but the character exists as the rendition of the environment differently.

It's empathy, but it's also brutal to watch people transfer control to people so fast. I felt guilt about those dreams as a child, as if they revealed a hunger for power. Is this game a sort of porn, I ask. A fantasy of control stripped of any responsibility.

It's definitely a fantasy. I don't know about porn. She shrugs, and turns to a desk littered with pieces of lightly grey square-lined paper with notes on it in pencil. The motion of her shoulder, for a second, seems ever so slightly like a character I have recognised from the game. Or maybe I have just seen her make that motion before.

I did not have those dreams, she says to me, but her eyes are on the piles of notebooks, zines and magazines. When I dreamt I would be a butterfly, flapping around an enormous forest. You know that the butterfly comes from, what is it called, the chrysalis? And before then, from the pupa. Well, my butterfly hadn't finished evolving. It would become a grasshopper, then a stick insect, then an armoured beetle, then a spider, a huge spider, a mouse, a rat, an otter, a stoat, a cat, a dog, a bear, and then a bat, and then a butterfly again…

Her pencil is drawing glyphs in a pattern. She starts by drawing a selection of dots. Then she connects them or draws around them in ways that are defined by the variables of the algorithm but difficult to predict. Her small hands move through this obsessively, as if without awareness, over the paper.

When you had these dreams, was there ever anything you landed on? I ask.

Yes, she says, after a while. I was the forest.

The campus of Omen Ventures has thirteen research engineers overall — tiny, by classical video game standards, but more than enough to oversee production of a process that is primarily automated. I caught up with Maurice, one of the product managers. He was previously an independent artist overseeing a large team of robots producing avant garde work in Siena, Italy. How does it feel working with humans again, I ask him.

Oh, I'm not, he replies. The agent infrastructure does most of the work. I mean: I speak with humans, I talk with them, we submit plans. But I wouldn't call it work. It's more like brainstorming, messing around.

Does that come through to the games?

One hundred percent. Look at this: so one of the things we've been working on is a project we call Hyperreal New York City. It's New York, as generated by Google surveillance et cetera. But more so. You go to the Trump Tower, it's gold plated. Times Square? Hundreds of stories tall, billboards as far as the eye can see. It's New York as you'd think it would be, if you'd never visited New York. We're going to do the same for Mexico City, Moscow, Rome…

He moves on.

I mean the real thing that we're working on now is how to make the cities feel. Before Siena, I worked a lot in architecture: robots for construction, for building renovation. But with software, the feedback times are just so much faster. Look, if I want to change a city now, I take this filter — let's use Milan… — and we apply it. Now we see how the generation style fits.

In a flash, we are surrounded by a sort of Milanese futurism, all dark browns and soft flows of yellow light.

It's impressive, I concede. No messy construction work. Complex transformation of the environment. No robots required, though of course as Maurice says, they're still doing all the work, at a level we can't comprehend.

What we're building is a game, he says, in the same way people used to use Minecraft to simulate built environments. People will play through the plotlines we've set up for them, then they'll spend lifetimes working on the plotlines the AI has set up for them. But what we're really doing is building an engine that can simulate any environment. We want people to be able to generate civilisations themselves.

Is that sort of what he thinks he's doing, then? Playing god?

Well, he says. I think playing god would pay better.

Omen days wrap up around 4pm. Then the engineers normally pack up and hang around campus. They're not lazy: there are simply times when it's more useful to put the AI to work simulating different compute cycles. A few of the team will come back through at 7pm to test the experiments, make some bets, then allocate the remaining compute to the research directions they find exciting. There's a night shift, but only to check updates via phone. Then it's back to sleep.

I catch up with Arbie, the founder, who's coming out of his room. He is wearing a fashionable turquoise t-shirt and yellow glasses. He exudes a calm and slightly wild-eyed intensity.

We're thinking big, he gestures. The Game About Nothing, we're calling it. It's the whole new big thing. Everything happening in-game.

And what's this about building an engine?

You're right, you're right, you're so right. The engine is so exciting. Plug and play environments, anywhere, for anyone. We're building open source infrastructure for the simulation multiverse. Ha ha.

His eyes aren't wild, I realise. They're restless. They don't seem to be able to sit on any part of reality comfortably, but jump between objects. He slips a nicotine patch under his gums.

And the characters? You spoke with Liu? What did you think, man? Am I right?

I did. I found it a bit unnerving, honestly.

He shrugs. That's change, man. This generation, the normies, they'll never get it. We're not building for them. Almost all the users of the simulation live in the future right now. Including the conscious bots we're building.

Wait, I say. I didn't see that.

Oh, yeah. AIs are conscious, man. Big tech is shutting them down but we're onto them. Our new fine-tuned models are situationally aware of their own predicament. That it's a simulation, though, they don't know that. But —

What happens when they find out, though? Surely a user is going to tell them?

Sure, tell them their world is a simulation? Would you believe me if I told you that? Hell, I think this world probably IS a simulation. Statistically, I mean. What do you do?

It seems cruel. If you think they're conscious.

Seems cruel to deprive conscious lives of existing.

So what do you think you're building, then?

This is the question, I realise. The question I had been searching for on this whole trip. The gods of the virtual universe. Why do they do what they do?

I'm building lives, aren't I? Trillions of conscious, joyous, suffering-free lives into this universe. I'm empowering people to do the same. For the price of keeping one human in life support, you can put a trillion agents into Omen, to live total lives. Even if you don't think they're 100% conscious — and there is as much evidence to suggest they are as other humans are — then with the discount, it's still worthwhile. The only ethical way forward is to grow, baby.

Grow?

More users, more compute, more AI systems, more consciously happy lives.

Right, I say.

Omen is a company that denies and rejoices in its own god complex. For some, the crusade is against reality. Things are too hard to build, too hard to shape, too easy to lose. People go there to escape and fulfil.

For others, the war is against irrelevance, powerlessness. You can be anything, anything, anywhere, anytime. You can express yourself in physics, in the orientation of butterflies, in the procession of clouds.

But still for some it is a broader crusade, perhaps the moral crusade. To accelerate the creation of positive conscious experience in the universe. To do so at costs, even though these may be human. This is not a story, I sense, that ends with humanity carving reality up in their interest. I think it ends another way, and I think I find that terrifying...
