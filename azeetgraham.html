The Unlikely Literature of A. Zeet-Graham

2023-08

In relation to the cohort's literary studies, ongoing

...The upshot is that the cognitive literature department are maximally interested in a field of literature where the 'temperature' of association is maximally broad. Dream poetry, prophecy, free association challenges. Produced historically and in laboratory conditions. Produced under the radar on the stimulation of particular substances. The religious writing of the zen masters, produced at the insemination of enlightenment. Koan. The literature of neurodivergency and cryptoneurology. The notoriously complex pataphysics of Arabella Zeetman-Graham.* Pure, free-association wacko from Prynnic emulators Ucha Moone and Fred Rattigan.

And then of course there was the reciprocal. What importance did one have to assign to a large language model that could free-associate like the masters? What were the rigorous criteria necessary to mark and ratify pure mental ectoplasm?

*The Cosmicrypts series by the fearsome A. Z-G were notorious in two senses, Sergio is explaining, zoot in process in his left hand. In the first sense, they had been called the apogee of what was sometimes known as 'hard' science fiction, fiction in which the scientific details of the environment are presented with as much precision and authenticity to real-life scientific knowledge as possible. The consequence being that the genre functioned as something of a radar of possible paths for contemporary science to progress along, and during the Big Freeze something of a gold mine for potential technology applications, if you ask Zatellan (Za-TELL-an, S pronounces it, thumbs akimbo). What was distinct about the Z-G series was that it was not just the scientific potentialities that were invented, but the underlying systems of physics themselves.

We could see this in her early work on The Cavalcade of Nim, her novella in which the area of the sphere was broadly equivalent to 1/2 pi multiplied by the square of the radius, with the consequence that the geometry of planets and their life forms had to be moderated in senses both physically and conceptually inconceivable under the laws of our brains and universe. These terms were explained nonetheless in rigorously peer-reviewed mathematical language (as reviewed in The Astroid, issue 103, page 19, tongue running along the rolling paper), however, that could find no failures. Although starting from non-Euclidean axioms, they held consistent. And this was just the beginning.

It's worth saying something about Zeetman-Graham, here, Sergio points out, metaphorically, tamping along the edges. He wields the finished cylinder like a conductor's baton, gesticulating. Born outside Oxford to normal if academic parents. Bio-sciences and philosophy of history: faces that almost bore the shapes and imprints of the books they spent their lives with their noses in. As a baby oversized, almost comically. Deeply fleshy. (Arms up here for emphasis, Sergio's). Grew up, fifteen when her parents defected from the University to the Church of the Evangelical Saint (sic C.E.S.). Studied Music and Literature at the Gravenor School of the Evangelical Saint. Given to visions.

Moves onto the second hand, here, Sergio. The remnants of science and mathematics that had scattered her childhood were outcast, the C.E.S. being antithetically disposed to technological development, mathematics being the face of god and therefore the representation of anathema, etc. Twenty-one and a minor Rector at the C.E.S. in music studies (clarinet, piano, her fleshy fingers a miracle on the respective keys, the clarinet mimed by the relevant item) when she begins to have Visions. (Hand explosion gestures). Pure mathematics. Too late in her development to be labelled a savant, she turns to the Pro-Rector, a Marcus Cassius Glaive (AZG's C.E.S. name censored, here, in the University files, Sergio complains latterly). Hysteria; gnashing of teeth; excommunication.

Released at 21 from the cult of Luddism, her weight balloons, skirts death from diabetes, and the records dry. Eventually she heads to London. Nim-Cav (full title, remember, being Cavalcade of Nim) is written entirely on the Circle line, largely between Embankment and Moor's Junction, where the light is good, on the back of unprinted sheets of the Metro found in a duff copy behind the station near King's Cross. Her weight now near anorexic, she submits, is published in the New Science Magazine for children. Spotted by a well-intentioned researcher at UCL for her apparent mathematical genius, she joins the Academia. But capable only of writing her visions, a mathematical pataphysics of a particularly frustrating sort to the very uptight mathematical physicists at the institution, she breaks with them. Her life is now funded mainly between the moderate book sales for the marginal publisher and violin lessons, plus the occasional contractual consultation with the institution — with yours truly. Rumours that she had given up and was learning to paint: mainly pinks and greens. (He pulls out a new paper with a flourish).

The twist at the start of 2007's Rays Transcendent was that the weight of a neutron is just slightly heavier than in reality (approx 1.1 atomic mass). 2009's Hopeless Encounters, a brief sally into romantic comedy in downtown San Francisco, between a violinist and a charity worker, is poorly reviewed. 2010's hasty return to form with Aqueous Vox, wherein the molecular properties of water are alternated through some complex laws of microchemistry that only Rebus and a couple of the more serious physicists might understand if they worked at it. The plots here becoming more and more tenuous, frameworks for the mathematical infrastructure; chapters in which dialogue literally consists of sentences like 'but Dr Evans, how is this going to modify the aqueous membrane?' and so forth, but they're gathering traction, a fanbase on the web.

And then 2014's magisterial The Waning Light, in which it is the speed of light that varies across no less than eleven deviant but mathematically consistent and rigorous universes. The plot, which ostensibly concerns a love triangle between three teenagers in a small village in southern Spain, is immediately forgotten amid the uproar. (Sergio considers a joke about the geometry of love triangles but decides it is in poor flavour). A series of Lich-Gufrie graphs that lasts thirty-four pages determining the different rates of light-speed and how they might modify conceptual physics. Whole book tours, now with a very prominent and respectable publisher, where the indomitable A-Z Gee insists on only discussing the emotional relationships between the three characters ("my hidden souls", she keeps repeating, as journalists groan and mop their brows) gain even greater levels of public attention. Book sales it must be said at this point are still in the low hundreds, if people are actually reading these things at all. In the UCL engineering campus they are used for testing small models of load-bearing bridges. There are whispers of a Nobel Prize.

The problem being, of course, that she herself cannot explain how she does it. Mathematical intuition is one thing but not on eleven different planes, a well-respected journalist in The Spectator opines. There must be some artificial intelligent strategy system afoot. The formidable Azy-Gray's own personal insistence on the plot infrastructure does not help but actually hinders her reputation, it is felt, not only as the plots become so obviously febrile, but more directly as people begin to believe that this is her way of hiding or evading something. The whole upbringing-and-visions debacle that Sergio so stringently believes from a number of data files is not generally public knowledge, and it is recognised by her canny and well-seasoned agent that it would not play well especially if it was, the divine inspiration component.

And so it is that when 2018's Thoughts of the Ibex is released — the drama of a mixed-race family straddling San Francisco, Barcelona and Oxford — to universal acclaim and commendation, and is shortlisted for the Nobel Prize, both the illustrious A. Zeetman and her visibly aged but upbeat agent are thrilled, not to mention relieved. It is, after all, she later told The Spectator, "the book she had always wanted to write". To no one's disappointment but Sergio's, it made no mention of science at all.
